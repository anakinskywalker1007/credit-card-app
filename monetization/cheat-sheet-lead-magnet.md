## How We Score Cards

Every card on CardTier gets a **Net Annual Value** score — what you actually pocket after annual fees, using average American spending patterns. We cut through the marketing fluff and show you the real math.

Simple formula: **Rewards Earned − Annual Fee + Credits & Perks = Your Actual Return**

The cards below aren't ads. They're the ones that consistently win on this math.

## 1. Chase Sapphire Preferred® — Best All-Around Starter Card

**Annual Fee:** $95
**Welcome Bonus:** 60,000 points (worth ~$750 in travel)
**Net Annual Value:** ~$400–$500 in year one

### Why It Wins
This is the gold standard for a reason. Three times points on dining, two times on travel, and Chase Ultimate Rewards points are among the most flexible in the game — you can transfer to United, Hyatt, Southwest, or just use them at 1.25¢ each in the Chase portal.

The $95 fee pays for itself in your first dinner out. The signup bonus alone covers nearly 8 years of annual fees.

### Best For
First serious rewards card. Travelers. Anyone who eats at restaurants (which is everyone).

### The Math
- Spend $2k/mo average → earn ~$720/yr in rewards
- Minus $95 fee = **$625 net annual value**
- Year one: add $750 bonus = **$1,375 total value**

## 2. American Express® Gold Card — Best for Foodies & Grocery Shoppers

**Annual Fee:** $250
**Welcome Bonus:** 60,000–75,000 points (varies, worth $750–$937 in Amex Travel)
**Net Annual Value:** ~$300–$450 after credits

### Why It Wins
Four times points at restaurants (worldwide). Four times at U.S. supermarkets (up to $25k/yr). If you cook or eat out, you're printing points.

The $250 fee sounds scary but $120 in dining credits (Grubhub, Cheesecake Factory, etc.) and $120 in Uber Cash effectively chop it down to $10. Transfer points to Delta, Marriott, or Hilton and they become worth 1.5–2¢+ each.

### Best For
People who spend $500+/mo on food between groceries and restaurants.

### The Math
- $500/mo food spend → 2,000 points/mo → 24,000 points/yr at 4x
- At 1.5¢/pt redemption = $360
- Plus credits worth ~$240 → **$600 in value on $250 fee = +$350 net**

## 3. Capital One Venture X — Best Premium Card (That Actually Makes Sense)

**Annual Fee:** $395
**Welcome Bonus:** 75,000 miles (~$750 value)
**Net Annual Value:** ~$300–$600 depending on usage

### Why It Wins
The Venture X is the premium card that doesn't require a PhD to justify. The $300 annual travel credit (booked through Capital One) wipes out most of the fee. Add the 10,000 bonus miles every anniversary (~$100) and you're effectively paying $0–$95 for a card with:

- 10x on hotels and rental cars through Capital One Travel
- 5x on flights through the portal
- 2x on everything else (unlimited — no caps)
- Priority Pass lounge access (for you AND authorized users for free)

### Best For
Moderate travelers who fly 2–4 times/year. People tired of juggling category bonuses.

### The Math
- $395 fee − $300 travel credit − $100 anniversary miles = **~$5 effective fee**
- 2x on everything else is just gravy

## 4. Bilt Mastercard® — Best Card if You Pay Rent

**Annual Fee:** $0
**Welcome Bonus:** No traditional signup bonus, but earns on rent from day one
**Net Annual Value:** $300–$600+ depending on rent

### Why It Wins
Nobody else lets you earn points on rent without a fee. Bilt does, and the points are elite — transferable to American Airlines, United, Hyatt, and more at 1:1 ratio.

Pay rent (up to $50k/yr). Earn 1x points. That's free points on the single biggest monthly expense in most budgets. The card also earns 3x on dining and 2x on travel.

One catch: you must make **at least 5 transactions per statement period** for rent to earn points.

### Best For
Renters in their 20s and 30s who haven't bought a home. Hyatt loyalists (Bilt → Hyatt = gold).

### The Math
- $1,500/mo rent = 18,000 points/yr just from rent
- At 1.7¢/pt (Hyatt transfer) = **$306/yr from rent alone — on a $0 fee card**

## 5. Citi Strata Premier℠ Card — The Most Underrated Card in the Industry

**Annual Fee:** $95
**Welcome Bonus:** 75,000 points after $4k spend in 3 months (~$750 value)
**Net Annual Value:** ~$400–$600

### Why It Wins
This card gets slept on constantly. It earns **3x on restaurants, groceries, gas, flights, AND hotels**. That's 5 major spending categories all at 3x — most cards only hit 2–3.

Citi ThankYou points transfer to Avianca LifeMiles, Turkish Miles&Smiles, and Singapore KrisFlyer. These are premium programs where savvy travelers routinely get 3–5¢ per point in business class value.

The $95 annual fee is identical to the Sapphire Preferred, but Strata covers more spend categories.

### Best For
People who drive + eat + travel. Strategic points collectors. Anyone who hasn't discovered Turkish Miles.

### The Math
- $4k/mo across covered categories → 12,000 points/mo → 144,000 pts/yr
- Even at 1¢/pt = $1,440 value on a $95 fee = **$1,345 net annual value**

# 🚫 THE 5 TO AVOID

---

## 1. First Premier Bank Credit Card — The Fee Trap

**Annual Fee:** $75–$125 *plus* monthly fees in year two
**Rewards:** Zero
**Why It's a Trap:** Marketed as a "credit builder" to people with bad/no credit. You pay $75–$125 just to *have* the card, often on top of a $25–$45 monthly maintenance fee after year one. No rewards. Low limits. You can build credit the same way with a secured card that costs nothing.

---

## 2. Credit One Bank Platinum Visa — Looks Free, Isn't

**Annual Fee:** $75 (buried in fine print, billed as $6.25/mo)
**Rewards:** 1% cashback on "eligible purchases"
**Why It's a Trap:** The annual fee eats your rewards before you even swipe. At 1% cashback, you'd need to spend $7,500 just to break even. Meanwhile, the Discover it® Secured earns 2% at restaurants and gas, and the Bilt card has zero fee.

---

## 3. Most Store Credit Cards (Kohl's, Gap, Macy's, etc.)

**APR:** 26–32%
**Rewards:** 5% off at one store
**Why It's a Trap:** Single-store utility, sky-high interest, and if you carry a balance even once, you've paid more in interest than you'll ever earn in Kohl's Cash. These cards exist to make retailers money, not you.

Exception: Store cards can make sense if you spend $3k+/yr at one retailer AND always pay in full. Most people don't.

---

## 4. Amex Platinum (for Most People) — Impressive Card, Wrong Audience

**Annual Fee:** $695
**Why Most People Should Skip It:** The Platinum's $695 fee is justified only if you actively use: Centurion Lounge access, $200 airline incidental credit, $200 hotel credit, $240 digital entertainment credit, $155 Walmart+ credit, and $100 Global Entry. That's 6 different credits across 5 vendors to manage. Most people use 2–3 and call it a win — which means they're paying $695 for a $200 card. The Venture X does 90% of what the Platinum does for $300 less.

---

## 5. Milestone® / Indigo® / Fingerhut Mastercards — Predatory Fee Structure

**Annual Fee:** $35–$99
**APR:** 24–36%
**Why It's a Trap:** These cards are aggressively marketed to people with credit scores under 580. They charge setup fees, annual fees, and sometimes monthly fees — all before you make a single purchase. Your $300 credit limit costs you $99 upfront. A secured card at any major bank (Discover, Capital One) gives you the same credit-building benefit with no annual fee.

# 🧠 THREE RULES FOR 2026

**Rule 1: The $95 test.** For a $95 annual fee card to make sense, it needs to return at least $200 in value. Both the Sapphire Preferred and Citi Strata clear this bar without trying.

**Rule 2: Credits are only real if you use them.** A card with $400 in credits you'll never use is worse than a card with $95 in straightforward cashback. Be honest about your spending habits before upgrading to a premium card.

**Rule 3: One card isn't a strategy.** The best setup for most people is a 2-card combo — one card with strong bonus categories (Amex Gold, Sapphire Preferred, or Strata Premier) paired with a flat-rate card for everything else (Venture X at 2x, or Citi Double Cash at 2%). That's it. You don't need 12 cards.
